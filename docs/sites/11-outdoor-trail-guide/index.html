△ Trail Knowledge

Trail Guide

Everything you need to know before, during, and after your adventure.

A well-packed bag is the difference between a great hike and a miserable one. For day hikes, aim for 20–35 L; overnights need 50–65 L.

  • 2 L of water per person (plus purification tablets)
  • High-calorie snacks — nuts, jerky, energy bars
  • Moisture-wicking base layer + insulating mid-layer
  • Waterproof shell jacket & rain pants
  • First-aid kit with blister treatment
  • Headlamp with spare batteries
  • Sun protection: SPF 50+, sunglasses, hat
  • Topographic map + compass (don't rely on phone)
  • Emergency whistle & signal mirror
  • Trekking poles for steep terrain

The wilderness rewards the prepared. Always prioritize safety over summit glory — the mountain will be there next season.

  • File a trip plan with a trusted contact before departing
  • Check weather forecasts the morning of your hike
  • Turn around by noon on exposed alpine routes
  • Never hike alone on technical terrain
  • Know the symptoms of altitude sickness: headache, nausea, confusion
  • Carry a PLB or satellite messenger on remote trails
  • Stay on marked trails to avoid getting lost
  • Make noise in bear country — bear spray mandatory in Grizzly zones

The seven principles of Leave No Trace are our code of ethics. We hold every guest to these standards to preserve wild places for generations to come.

  • Plan ahead: Know the rules and prepare for hazards
  • Travel on durable surfaces: Stick to established trails
  • Dispose of waste properly: Pack it in, pack it out
  • Leave what you find: No picking flowers or moving rocks
  • Minimize campfire impact: Use a camp stove where possible
  • Respect wildlife: Observe from a distance, never feed animals
  • Be considerate of others: Keep noise low, yield on trail

Each season offers a completely different wilderness experience. Here's what to expect throughout the year at elevation.

  • Spring (Apr–Jun): Wildflowers bloom, snowmelt waterfalls, muddy trails — microspikes essential above treeline
  • Summer (Jul–Aug): Peak season — warm days, afternoon thunderstorms common above 8,000 ft; summit early
  • Fall (Sep–Oct): Golden larches, crisp air, dramatic skies — best photography window of the year
  • Winter (Nov–Mar): Snowshoe and ski touring access only; avalanche beacon, probe & shovel required
